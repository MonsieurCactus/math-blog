<style type="text/css"> blockquote a { color: #BA5215;} </style> # Equidistribution of $p = a^2 + b^2 = e^{i\theta}$ --- <iframe width="560" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/RxI3BemTjfk/t=2400" frameborder="0" allowfullscreen></iframe> --- Fermat showed that just like $5 = 2^2 + 1^2 $ and $41 = 4^2 + 5^2$ prime number can be written as the sum of two squares $$p = a^2 + b^2$$ for any prime number $p = 4k+1$. The first few are easy to compute by hand, but try to write: $$ p = 817,504,253 = a^2 + b^2 \tag{$\ast$}$$ where $x$ and $y$ are integers. When you are done with that look at the [Prime Pages](http://primes.utm.edu/lists/small/millions/) or feel free to generate your own list. There are a few proofs in Hardy and Wright's [An Introduction to the Theory of Numbers](.) Here is one: > If $p = 4k+1$ then $(\frac{p-1}{2})!^2 = -1 \mod p$ this is connected to WilsoÅ„ s theorem and Fermat's little theorem. At least we know $x^2 = -1 \mod p$ has a solution, $x = \sqrt{-1} \in \mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$. > <br><br> > Then consider the lattice generated by $(x,1), (p,0) \in \mathbb{Z}^2$. The area of the fundamental domain is $p$. The circle $x^2 + y^2 = p$ has area $\pi p > p$, so there is a point of this lattice inside the circle. > <br><br> > The lattice point $(a,b)\neq (0,0)$ closest to the origin inside of this circle must lie exactly on the circle, so $a^2 + b^2 = p$. While, after reading this I still have no idea how to find $(a,b)$ technically this is still a proof. --- Don Zagier published a 1-sentence proof using an involution on the set $\\{(x,y,z): x^2 + 4yz = p \\} $ &#91; [1](http://mathoverflow.net/questions/31113/zagiers-one-sentence-proof-of-fermats-theorem), [2](http://people.mpim-bonn.mpg.de/zagier/files/doi/10.2307/2323918/fulltext.pdf) &#93; It is a run-on sentence simplifying work of Heath-Brown &#91; [3](http://eprints.maths.ox.ac.uk/677/1/invariant.pdf) &#93; The style is so different, maybe we can treat it separately. # Proofs that if $p = 4k+1 = a^2 + b^2 $ --- In addition to the proofs in Hardy and Wright, this result is stated and proved in a number of places. * Proofs related to Factorization in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$ or to continued fractions * http://math.stackexchange.com/questions/594/how-do-you-prove-that-a-prime-is-the-sum-of-two-squares-iff-it-is-congruent-to-1 * http://math.stackexchange.com/questions/5877/efficiently-finding-two-squares-which-sum-to-a-prime * Constructive Proofs of Fermat's Theorem * http://math.stackexchange.com/questions/74132/explicit-formula-for-fermats-4k1-theorem?lq=1 * Related "sum of two squares problems* * $p = 3k+1$ and $p = x^2 + xy + y^2 $ http://math.stackexchange.com/questions/782166/p-is-an-odd-prime-number-where-p-3k1-longleftrightarrow-exists-a-b-in-bbb-z?lq=1 * Vararious write-ups from number theory courses * Conrad http://www.math.uconn.edu/~kconrad/blurbs/ugradnumthy/Picksumofsq.pdf * Euler http://eulerarchive.maa.org/docs/translations/E241tr.pdf * the proof by [Heath-Brown](http://eprints.maths.ox.ac.uk/677/1/invariant.pdf) and the shorter proof by [Zagier](http://mathoverflow.net/questions/31113/zagiers-one-sentence-proof-of-fermats-theorem) are so different, that we discuss them later. The goal of collecting so many proofs of the Fermat two squares theorem is that we would ultimately like to study the distribution of the angle $p = a^2 + b^2 = (a+bi)(a-bi) $ with $a+bi = e^{i\theta}\sqrt{a^2 + b^2} $. This picture is convincing that the angles of the Gaussian primes ( in $\mathbb{Z}[i]$) are equidistributed as $p \to \infty$.
