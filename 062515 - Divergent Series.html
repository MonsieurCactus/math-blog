We have some compact operator $A$ acting on a Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}$, then it has eigenvalues $\\{ \lambda\_i \\}\_{i \in \mathbb{Z}} $ and eigenvectors $\\{ |\phi\_i \rangle\\}_{i \in \mathbb{Z}} $. Then using the spectral theorem I am inclined to write: $$ A = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} \lambda_n |\phi _n \rangle \langle \phi_n | $$ A simple choice could be the Laplacian, let $\mathcal{H} = L^2 (S^1)$ and $A = \frac{d^2}{d\theta^2}$, then $\lambda_n = n^2$ and $\langle \theta | \phi_n \rangle = e^{in\theta}$. $$ \frac{d^2}{d\theta^2} = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} n^2 | \phi_n \rangle \langle \phi_n | $$ There is something horribly wrong with this, because in fact my choice of $A$ is not-compact and $n^2 \not \to 0$. --- If we can Fourier expand the dirac delta function using the theory of distributions can we also Fourier expand the derivative? $$ \delta''(\theta) = \sum_{n \in \mathbb{Z}} n^2 e^{in\theta}$$ The meaning of the Dirac delta is somewhat delicate, but we can still plot, here $|n| < 10$. ![enter image description here][1] Although this was intended to be a discussion of the zeta function, we have generated an evaluated a number of interesting zeta functions.
